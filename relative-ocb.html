When Good Deeds Backfire: How Followers’ Relative OCB Can Trigger Unethical Favoritism

In organizations, employees often go the extra mile to support their supervisors—helping with tasks, providing advice, or showing loyalty. These actions, called organizational citizenship behaviors (OCB), are usually seen as positive. But what if good deeds could sometimes backfire?

Our recent research explored this question by studying a new concept: relative OCB toward the supervisor (ROCBS). Unlike traditional OCB, ROCBS measures how a particular employee’s supportive actions compare to other team members. In other words, it looks at a follower’s standing within the team in terms of how much they help or support their supervisor.

Key Findings

  • Relative OCB shapes perceived support: Supervisors noticed when certain employees consistently went above and beyond compared to others. This elevated perception of support became a powerful signal for supervisors.
  • Supervisor insecurity can trigger unethical favoritism: When supervisors were uncertain about their own competence, high perceived support often led them to show unethical favoritism behavior (UFB).
  • No favoritism when confidence is high: If supervisors were confident in their abilities, ROCBS and perceived support did not lead to UFB.

Lessons for Organizations

  • Monitor team dynamics: recognize and reward contributions fairly without creating dependency on certain employees.
  • Strengthen leader confidence: training, coaching, and clear performance standards can reduce the likelihood that supervisors act unethically.
  • Clarify ethical boundaries: employees’ helpful behaviors are valuable, but organizations must ensure that support does not become a reason for preferential treatment.
  • Balance recognition: promote a culture where contributions are appreciated collectively, not just individually.

Bottom Line

Even positive behaviors like citizenship support can have unintended consequences. By measuring relative OCB, organizations can better understand team dynamics and design interventions that maximize benefits while minimizing ethical risks.
